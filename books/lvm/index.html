About the Book

They are not doing it on purpose. That thing your partner does that drives you mad - the forgetting, the shutting down, the meltdowns over small things, the way they seem to be having a different conversation than you - they are not trying to hurt you. And you are not crazy for being frustrated. You are not too demanding. You are not imagining things.

You are in a neurodiverse relationship, one where ADHD, autism, or both are shaping everything that happens between you. And until you understand that, you will keep trying solutions that were never going to work.

Loving Variant Minds explains how neurodivergent brains actually work, why they create specific patterns in relationships, and what couples can realistically do about it. It covers ADHD and autism in depth, including AuDHD - when someone has both - and the particular complexity that combination creates. It addresses late diagnosis: why it happens, how it destabilises relationships, and what the path forward looks like when you discover your partner's neurology only after years of misunderstanding have already accumulated.

The book is honest about difficulty. Masking and what happens when it drops. Emotional dysregulation and its impact on the partner who witnesses it. The difference between what someone cannot change and what they can. Where understanding should lead to accommodation, and where it should lead to boundaries instead. These are not comfortable topics, but they are necessary ones.

This is not a book that promises everything will be fine if you just try harder or love more. Love alone is not enough - that is one of its central arguments. But love combined with understanding, with strategy, with the willingness to build structures that fit the brains you actually have rather than the brains you wish you had - that combination can work. This book shows you how.

About the Author

Silvino Rodrigues spent most of his adult life believing that his struggles were character flaws - failures of effort, discipline, or empathy. It was not until his fifties that dual diagnoses of autism and ADHD reframed everything: decades of professional reinvention, the exhaustion and disconnection he could never explain, and a marriage that slowly degraded under the weight of traits neither partner knew how to name.

This book grew from that wreckage. Drawing on three decades of corporate experience, postgraduate studies in psychology and adult education, and the particular clarity that comes from understanding something only after it's too late, Silvino offers what he wishes someone had given him: a map of the terrain, drawn by someone who walked it without one.

He now dedicates his time to neurodivergent research and advocacy.
